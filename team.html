Purpose

Our mission

Make high-frequency wireless links reliable enough for real-time applications. As the industry pushes toward mmWave and THz bands, the coherence-cliff problem will only grow worse.

We believe reliability should be designed in, not patched after failure. WaveLynk provides a physics-grounded framework for predictive reliability control.

Roadmap

  • Phase 1 ✓ — Theoretical framework, instability model, proxy experiment
  • Phase 2 — mmWave testbed validation (28 GHz SDR platform)
  • Phase 3 — Multi-user MIMO extension (>4 users)
  • Phase 4 — Real-time embedded controller for commercial APs

FAQ

Common questions

What exactly is the "Coherence Cliff"?

A nonlinear instability transition in ZF beamforming that occurs when CSI age, amplified by channel conditioning (κ), causes the inversion-based weights to become destructive. The result is a sudden jump in interference, latency, and packet loss.

How is this different from standard rate adaptation?

Standard rate adaptation reacts to observed degradation (low SNR, high loss). WaveLynk uses a physics-based predictive metric (CCI) that detects when the system is approaching the instability boundary before failure occurs.

Why is this a proxy experiment?

mmWave/THz hardware was not available. The underlying physics (Doppler shift, coherence time, channel conditioning) behaves identically — it scales with frequency. Our results demonstrate the mechanism; mmWave validation would strengthen the case.

What would you do with more resources?

Validate at 28 GHz with an SDR platform, use full channel matrix estimation for true κ, test with >4 simultaneous users, run >1000 trials per condition, and integrate with commercial AP firmware.

What's the biggest limitation?

The proxy nature of the experiment. We demonstrate the mechanism at 5 GHz, not at mmWave/THz frequencies where the coherence cliff is most severe. The physics scales, but direct validation at higher frequencies is needed.
