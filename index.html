Physics foundation

From E&M to the Coherence Cliff Instability Model

This project is grounded in electromagnetic wave physics: the wireless channel is the superposition of time-varying complex phasors created by propagating fields. CSI aging causes phase errors; ZF can amplify those errors when the channel is ill-conditioned.

Optional: E&M foundation (click to expand)
Maxwell’s Equations (fields → waves)

∇·E = ρ/ε0,   ∇·B = 0,   ∇×E = −∂B/∂t,   ∇×B = μ0J + μ0ε0∂E/∂t

In free space (ρ≈0, J≈0), these lead to the electromagnetic wave equation: ∇²E = μ0ε0 ∂²E/∂t² and c = 1/√(μ0ε0).

Complex phasor channel model

A narrowband MIMO channel entry can be modeled as a sum of paths:

h(t) = ∑p ap(t)\,e^{j\phip(t)}

Motion changes path lengths → phases rotate. The dominant phase drift rate is captured by Doppler:

fD ≈ v/λ,   λ = c/fc

Coherence time (how fast phase stays valid)

Coherence time is the time scale over which the channel remains correlated:

Tc ≈ 0.423 / fD

If CSI is “aged” by τ, then a simple phase-error scale is:

ΔΦ ≈ 2π fDτ

Why ZF collapses suddenly (conditioning amplification)

ZF depends on matrix inversion. When the channel matrix H is ill-conditioned, inversion amplifies small CSI errors. A standard perturbation bound is:

‖δH−1‖ / ‖H−1‖ ≤ κ(H) · ‖δH‖ / ‖H‖

Interpreting \u03B4H as phase drift from CSI aging gives a physics-driven collapse boundary.

κ · (2π fDτ) ≥ 1   →   Coherence Cliff (instability transition)

Intuition: higher frequency → larger fD (for the same motion) → shorter Tc, and high κ makes ZF more fragile.

Predictive index used in WaveLynk

We combine coherence physics and conditioning into a single risk score:

CCI = κ · (τ/Tc)

WaveLynk triggers a proactive switch (ZF→MRT) when CCI approaches the instability transition.

Clarifying the framework

Instability model vs. Coherence Cliff

Instability Model (Mathematical Boundary)

κ · (2π fD τ) ≥ 1

This equation defines the predictive boundary where CSI phase error, amplified by matrix conditioning (κ), becomes unstable. It explains why collapse happens.

Coherence Cliff (Observed Phenomenon)

The coherence cliff is the physical system behavior that occurs once the instability boundary is crossed — characterized by rapid interference growth, latency spikes, and packet loss.

The model predicts the boundary; the cliff describes the outcome.

ISEF Upgrade • Formalization

Coherence Cliff: Formal Instability Model

We model collapse as a conditioning-amplified phase error instability. ZF beamforming depends on matrix inversion. When the channel matrix becomes ill-conditioned (high κ), even small CSI phase drift can be amplified dramatically.

Instability boundary: κ · (2π fD τ) ≥ 1

Here, fD represents Doppler shift, τ is CSI age, and κ is the channel condition number. When their product crosses unity, interference leakage increases rapidly — producing abrupt latency and packet loss collapse.

Option 2

Conditioned Coherence Index (CCI)

CCI = κ · (τ / Tc)

We extend classical coherence time analysis by incorporating conditioning. This transforms CSI age into a real-time predictive risk score.

CCI < 0.5: Stable
0.5 – 1: Degrading alignment
> 1: Collapse regime

Option 3

Frequency Scaling Principle

As carrier frequency increases (5 GHz → 140 GHz), wavelength shrinks. For the same motion velocity, Doppler shift increases, reducing coherence time.

Higher f → Higher fD → Lower Tc → Higher CCI

This explains why mmWave/6G systems are significantly more vulnerable to CSI aging and beamforming instability.

Reproducibility protocol

Goal: demonstrate a controlled regime transition and validate that WaveLynk’s switch reduces outage behavior under matched conditions.

  1. Setup: router + 2–3 client devices. Fix channel/band, distance, antenna placement.
  2. Controls: same traffic pattern, same environment, repeated trials (n≥5).
  3. Measurements: RSSI (or received power proxy), ping latency, packet loss, throughput.
  4. Stressors: add controlled blockage, add motion, add multi‑user traffic (MU load).

Judge Q&A (fast answers)

Is this just switching schemes?

No. The novelty is the predictive threshold (CIN and Risk) that anticipates collapse — the switching is the control action driven by that predictor.

How do you know it’s not random noise?

We use controls + repeated trials, and show a consistent regime transition tied to measurable channel degradation (received power proxy, mobility, multi‑user interference).

Why would this matter for 6G/THz?

At higher frequency the channel changes faster and becomes more sensitive to micro‑motion/blockage, so CSI staleness and abrupt breakdown are more likely. A proactive controller becomes essential.

What is α?

α captures environment sensitivity: how quickly phase errors grow for a given CIN. In practice it can be learned from calibration trials; in the demo it’s adjustable so judges can see the effect.

Core Discovery: The Coherence Cliff

WaveLynk’s central discovery is that ZF beamforming does not fail gradually under CSI aging. Instead, it enters a nonlinear instability regime once channel information becomes too stale. To make this predictable rather than reactive, we introduced a normalized instability metric.

Key equations at a glance
Instability onset: κ · (2π fD τ) ≥ 1
Predictive index: CCI = κ · (τ/Tc)
Frequency scaling: f↑ → Tc↓ → risk↑
Control action: proactive switch ZF → MRT before collapse

1. Coherence Instability Number (CIN)

We define the Coherence Instability Number as:

CIN = τf / τc

τf represents effective feedback delay, and τc is coherence time. When CIN approaches 1, the channel has changed enough that inversion-based precoding becomes unreliable.

2. Why the Collapse Happens

Under ZF, matrix inversion amplifies CSI errors. Phase-error variance grows approximately as:

Var(Δφ) ∝ CIN²

Because this growth is quadratic, instability accelerates. This nonlinear transition region is what we define as the Coherence Cliff.

3. Controller Logic

Risk = α · CIN

When Risk approaches 1, WaveLynk proactively switches from ZF to MRT, avoiding entry into the collapse region and preventing sudden latency spikes.

Advanced Theoretical Extension

For deeper analysis, we can express instability in terms of explicit CSI error magnitude ε:



ε = || H − Ĥ ||F

Under inversion sensitivity:


PCCI(ε) ∝ ε²
SINR(ε) ≈ Ps / (aε² + N0)
εc ≈ √(N0 / a)

This research-level formulation mathematically explains the same nonlinear behavior operationally captured by CIN ≈ 1.
